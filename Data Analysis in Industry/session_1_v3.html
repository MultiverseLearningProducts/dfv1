The Data Analytics Lifecycle
We have considered what happens to a piece of data, but what about a project? Today's session is about helping you understand how to plan out a project that will deliver data driven insights. The data analytics lifecycle is a framework which all your projects are based around.
The data analytics life cycle governs every aspect of your project from its genesis to final completion. You should refer to each of these steps throughout (especially when writing up your portfolio). It is important to point out this life cycle flows both ways. It is common to go back a step when you realise something is missing from your data, or you need to readjust your plan due to an unforseen issue. Be flexible in your project and learn from everything you do to optimise your practice.

What is the context of business problem?

Why does the problem matter?

Who are the stakeholders?

What assumptions have you made about the problem?

What is your proposed solution and issues you have considered?

What data will you require?

The data analytics life cycle begins with planning out your project- what is the business problem that needs to be solved? What is the context of the problem? What is your proposed solution? What data will you need to achieve this? Do you have any other assumptions?

What are the data types?

What features are in your data?

Next comes data prep. 80% of your time on a data project will be spent on this section, checking for null values, missing values, data types, outliers, errors and more. A project lives or dies on the quality of its data, you need to ensure it is in the optimal shape.

blank

What aggregates have you calculated?

How have you visualised the data?

What trends have you observed?

Are there any correlations between features?

What insights have you gained from the data?

Once you have prepared the data you analyse it. You calculate aggregates (means and medians, counts, maximum and minimums) and visualise the data. You get an understanding of trends, correlations and an understanding of what features will be the most influential when building a predictive model (if that is what you are doing)

blank

What is your solution to the business problem?

Is it a prototype?

Based off your analysis, how have you come to this solution?

Next you build your model. This might be a machine learning model, a dashboard or a prototype. Whatever it is, it will be based on your previous analysis and aims to fulfil the business requirements you set out from the planning stage

blank

Have you considered other solutions?

How do you know yours is the best one?

How have you optimised your solution?

How have you verified that the project requirements have been met?

Once the model is built, it is time to refine and compare. When building models it is rare that you would only make one, and you may have considered a variety of visualisations for your dashboard. It is in this stage that you optimise what you have built and decide upon which version meets your project aims best.

blank

How have you presented your solution to the stakeholders?

What are your recommendations?

What are the next steps or future opportunities gained from this project?

Finally you communicate your findings. You go back to the key stakeholders and inform them of your data driven insight so they can make the best business decision. All work in your portfolio should demonstrate that your analysis has been communicated to someone, whether it is a model or visualisation.

Identifying Stakeholders
The remainder of this session will be around planning your project and first we will need to discuss stakeholders.
What is a stakeholder?
Ask the group to annotate the screen with their ideas. Discuss a few of them.
Who could be a stakeholder?
Ask the class to suggest their ideas for who can be a stakeholder. Draw on the idea that it could be anyone.

Stakeholders are individuals or groups who are involved in your project from whom you require input, or who will be influenced or impacted by the results.

A stakeholder is anyone who has a say in your project
Sponsor Team Users Suppliers
Examples: Directors, Senior Leadership, Heads of Department Examples: Project Managers, Team Leads, Line Managers, Sales Teams Examples: Customers, Clients, Business Owner Examples: Contractors, Consultants, Subject Matter Experts
Almost anyone can be a stakeholder but we can simplify them into four groups
  • Sponsors: those who issue the project, like those who you report into
  • Team: those who are on the same level as you and who you have to collaborate with
  • Users: the clients or customers who will make use of your output. Note a user and a customer may be different- a customer pays for the product whereas a user 'uses' it, for example a company will pay for a service while the employees use it.
  • Suppliers: those whose input is required for the project to be completed (could be an external data source or subject expert)
For each stakeholder you need to consider how involved they will be with your project and therefore what influence they will have, the next few slides will help structure this process.

1. What motivates them?

2. How can you influence them?

3. What information would they like to receive?

4. How would they like to receive it?

5. What information do we require from them?

6. What dependencies are they responsible for?

Dealing with Stakeholders
Let's think a bit more closely how we deal with stakeholders, how should we communicate with them?

Do you have enough information to draft a project brief to share with stakeholders?

Are you accurately capturing the necessary information through effective questioning?

The first thing to consider is if we understand their requirements. Requirements elicitation is a key aspect of a project where you capture their needs so you can draw up a project brief (will be discussed in more detail later). A key aspect of this is the questions you ask.
A good question is one that lets you obtain the type, quality and quantity of the information you need
Effective questionning will allow you to understand precisely what the stakeholder wants and therefore what you need to deliver. It will also help you determine how involved they will need to be and what tools you will likely need to use. A competently led interview will also assure the stakeholder of your expertise.
Open ended questions encourage people to reflect and reveal what is important to them
When asking questions it is better to ask open ended questions to encourage reflection. Closed questions can be useful in guiding someone towards a response so if a stakeholder does not know what they want then you should mix your closed and open questions to stimulate discussion.
Type Desired Outcome Example
Explorative Expand on new points of view and uncovered areas Have you thought of...?
Affective Reveal stakeholder's feelings about something How do you feel about...?
Reflective Encourage elaboration What do you think causes...?
Probing Invite a deeper examination Can you describe how...?
Analytical Find the root of a problem What are the causes of...?
Clarifying Help align and avoid misunderstandings You mean that...?
You should also mix up the types of questions you are asking. Plan out your questions in advance, thinking about what you want to achieve. Stakeholders may be busy and not be able to devote a lot of time to thinking about your project. Your questions then will help elicit the required information. Communication Plan

To help keep track of stakeholders you should maintain a communication plan to help you:

  • Determine how often you will be in contact with stakeholders
  • Decide on your messages (ignore, follow, inform, consult, involve, etc)
  • Decide on the medium of contact (email, phone call, etc)
  • Assign a responsible project member
Something you will need to decide with your stakeholders is how often you intend to communicate with them. Major stakeholders will require regular updates, while others can be informed once the project is complete. A communication plan can help you keep track of this.

Business Need for Analysis
Data analytics is used in business to help organisations make better business decisions.
Whether it’s market research, product research, positioning, customer reviews, sentiment analysis, or any other issue for which data exists, analysing data will provide insights that organisations need in order to make the right choices.
Data analytics is important for businesses today, because data-driven choices are the only way to be truly confident in business decisions. Some successful businesses may be created on a hunch, but almost all successful business choices are data-based.
When writing your project briefs you will need to be clear on what type of analysis you are going to do, and in your portfolio explain why it is the appropriate choice. The next few slides will cover 4 main types of analytics.

Prescriptive Analytics

 

Looking at past data to tell what has happened

E.g. KPIs, revenue, sales leads

The first type of analytics is descriptive, where you look to summarise or describe the data. In this type of analysis you are looking for insights from past data to understand issues like performance and how you are progressing towards targets. Key performance indicators are a common type of this analysis.

Descriptive Analytics

Diagnostic Analytics

Predictive Analytics

Prescriptive Analytics

 

Determining the cause of an observed event

Diagnostic analysis is about uncovering the root cause of what you observed in your descriptive analysis. For example, you observe revenue increased in October- why did that happen? Which marketing effort contributed the most? Diagnostic analysis can be about investigating positive or negative occurrences and trying to work out what caused them so you can make necessary adjustments.

Descriptive Analytics

Diagnostic Analytics

Predictive Analytics

Prescriptive Analytics

 

Predicting what will happen in the future

In this type of analysis you will look at past trends in your data to make predictions in the future. This could be using time series forecasting or building basic machine learning regression or classification models. For example, to predict next year's revenue you will need to analyse trends from the past few years to build a picture of what is happening in the market. Predictive analytics can be applied to very complex issues such as risk assessment, sales forecasting and qualifying leads.

Descriptive Analytics

Diagnostic Analytics

Predictive Analytics

Prescriptive Analytics

 

Creating an action plan based off your analysis

Combining the other three types of analysis together gives prescriptive analytics. This is when you use your analysis to give stakeholders your recommendation on what should be done- this is where data-driven choices are made. For example, you could find from your diagnostic analysis that there is an issue with a product so you recommend a plan to boost sales. Or, you may have built a model to predict customers defaulting on loans, so you advise stakeholders on which customers are most likely to default so they can for a plan. Whatever type of analytics you plan to do, you should include this in your project brief and portfolio.

Verfication


The internal process of evaluating a data project to determine whether the products of a given development phase satisfy the conditions imposed at the start. This includes:

  • Inspecting
  • Design Analysis
  • Specification Analysis
Before we finish, we need to discuss verification and validation. Two industry terms which seem similar but you need to able to describe in your portfolio. Verification occurs when you want to check if your project has met the requirements of a given phase- for example, does your dashboard show the correct information? Does it work as intended? Is your data pipeline accessible? Whenever you are ready to move onto the next phase of a project you should verify that the success conditions have been met. In other words, have you done the project correctly?

Validation


The external process of evaluating a data project during or at the end of the development process to determine whether it satisfies specific external requirements. This includes:

  • Evaluating if the product meets the client expectations and requirements
Validation occurs when you want to check if external requirements are met. For example, does the dashboard you have built integrate with the client's data infrastructure? If you are creating a data pipeline, is the data you are extracting conform to the correct format? We will look into these two concepts more closely in the next session when looking at ETL. In other words, have you done the correct project? In your portfolio, you need to point out when you have performed verification or validation, why you did it and what you concluded.
