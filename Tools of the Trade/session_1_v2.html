Objective: Understand the characteristics that make up 'Big Data.'

Our current definition of Big Data is that it is too big for your computer, but let's consider a more robust approach to defining it. Industry experts refer to the 5 v's as metrics to evaluate whether data is Big or not. These are volume, velocity, variety, veracity and value. We will go through each of these in turn.

Volume


  • Refers to the size of the data

  • Whether a dataset is considered 'big' or not is dependent on the volume

  • Data is considered Big when it is too voluminous for traditional data storage methods

  • Big Data platforms provide a way to store incredibly large amounts of data as well the ability to process it efficiently
The most obvious metric is its volume- how much data there is. If it is too big for your computer to handle (like Peta or Exa-bytes) then it is Big. 20 years ago Gigabytes would have been considered 'Big.' Ask apprentices to annotate the screen to suggest challenges that can occur due to the size of data (e.g. processing time, storage limits, etc). Also ask them to consider the volume of the data they use regularly.

Velocity


  • Refers to the speed of data generation

  • For example, how fast data flows in from sources such as application logs, networks and social media sites

  • Typically the flow will be massive and continuous
Velocity refers to the speed of data generation, twitter feeds and stock markets generate data incredibly quickly and what is 'fresh' can go out of data within minutes. 'Big Data' will typically be massive and continuous and will require powerful processors to keep up. What challenges can we think of that arise from the velocity of our data? What are the potential benefits of harnessing fast flowing data?

Veracity


  • Refers to the quality of the data

  • Checking the veracity of data sources is always challenging- never trust data as given

  • Enquire about the data preparation- has it been normalised already? Has it been subject to statistical manipulation (i.e. outliers removed)?

  • Where possible you should always ask to look at raw data
This refers to the quality of the data. What pitfalls can arise due to poor quality data? These issues are much worse with Big Data as there is so much of it, and any errors can lead to catastrophic failures which can be both expensive and time consuming. You should always have checks then for your incoming data- has it been normalised? Has it been cleaned? Have the structures been checked and processes put in place to deal with them? Never assume data is clean! As Big Data often comes from a disparate variety of sources, this can be challenging- especially when you also have to consider issues around ownership, GDPR and company data policies. Considering how expensive it can be to use Big Data, you do not want to find your analysis has been ruined due to issues around data quality- it could be both a frustrating and expensive mistake.

  • Refers to the business value of the data

  • Having access to vast amounts of fast flowing data is useless unless it can leveraged into something of value

  • How does this data add value to your business? What is the ROI?
Finally the value of the data. It is all well and good to have access to Big Data, but it is pointless if you cannot leverage it into something valuable like making a profit. You should always ask what the added value or return on investment is when considering using Big Data. Consider also that utilising Big Data technologies can be expensive (as unless you own the server you will have to rent it) as well as the environmental impact of these remote servers working at high capacity. Can you justify your use of Big Data when considering how much it will cost?

Alexa follows a simple (but complicated) process where it learns from what you say and to provide the output. The slide below walks through the process in more detail.
  1. Your words are recorded
  2. The recording is sent to Amazon's server to be analysed (due to interpreting sounds taking up alot of computational power
  3. Important words are identified and corresponding functions are carried out (e.g. 'basketball' would open a sports app)
  4. The server then sends the information back to your device. If Alexa needs to say something it will follow a reverse process (identify the words it wants to give and convert them to the correct sounds)

It would then return these results to you, via the server where it will have worked out what 'sounds' (words) it will need to speak to you.
How it Works
Objective: Understand the principles behind how Big Data technologies aid our analysis.
In this section we will be examing how Big Data technology can speed up our analysis.

Divide and Conquer

Most Big Data softwares use an approach called 'Divide and Conquer' where the data will be split into smaller 'chunks' and processed simultaneously across different nodes and the results combined. This process happens over several servers so if one fails the analysis can continue and the remaining can pick up the slack.

Has anyone ever tried to run analysis on a dataset that runs into millions of rows? How long did it take? When using Excel or a Jupyter Notebook the processing is being done by your computer. There are very powerful personal computers you can buy, but these are often optimised for computer games. Most of the computers we use are only powerful enough to handle a few Gigabytes which severly limits how quickly (and how much) data can be processed.
This is where Big Data technologies come to the rescue. Instead of running your algorithm on your computer, you send the data to a remote server (run by a Big Data company) where your algorithm/script will run. The server uses an approach called 'divide and conquer' where it will share the data across several nodes and process them seperately before combining the results and sending them back. When you consider how powerful the processors these servers have you can see how much faster your analysis will be completed when using Big Data technology. This is especially true when the data is in large volume and fast moving, with Big Data servers able to store huge quantities and even have capabilities to deal with different data types and structures. Another advantage of spreading the data across different nodes is that if one fails for whatever reason, the others can pick up the slack. Compare this to running analysis on your computer- if it loses power midway through a large piece of analysis you will lose everything.

Which is Faster?

Let's illustrate how much quicker processing can be using Divide and Conquer. Imagine you have to transfer 1 Terabyte of data (1000 GB) between two computers. You have access to a 2TB removable hard drive and ten 128 GB USB sticks. Assuming upload/download speeds are constant and both computers have at least 10 USB ports which method will be quicker? Give apprentices a few minutes to attempt before showing the solutions.

Ask the apprentices for their answers (can use annotate or chat function) before revealing solution. Note that the second method is 10 times faster. This shows how much quicker dividing your processing up can be when using Big Data technologies. However, sending your data to a Big Data server can be expensive as you are effectively 'renting' someone else's processors. What you need to weigh up is the cost of using the technology against the added value of processing larger amounts of data more quickly and efficiently (again when you consider the capabilities of dealing with different data types). Something else to consider is accuracy- you do not want to make a mistake. Sending some data to the cloud to find that you had made a syntax error or some of the data was corrupt (or not cleaned properly) will not only be frustrating but potentially expensive to your employer as well.

Advantages and Disadvantages
Objective: Justify the use of Big Data technologies in analysis by considering the benefits and drawbacks.
In this section you will be leading a discussion on the advantages of using (or not using) Big Data. The aim is for apprentices to justify their decision for project write ups in their portfolio.

Advantages

  • Ability to access and process large volumes of data quickly
  • Analysis run on larger datasets will be more reliable, representative and accurate as taking samples from data will not be necessary
  • Faster analysis leads to a reduction in human utilisation, meaning more projects can be undertaken or better resourcing of analysts
  • Projects finished in shorter time frames can lead to increased reputation amongst customers and products being brought to market
Read through the advantages on the screen. Can apprentices think of any more? Ask them to think about how they could incorporate these justifications in their portfolio in the context of their business problems. Using Big Data technologies can lead to companies leveraging better insights through processing larger quanities of data in a timely manner. This can lead to better products, increased engagement from customers and employees moving onto more interesting projects. The advantages are there, but there are some pitfalls to consider too...

Disdvantages

  • It is expensive
  • Big Data technologies only provide a small amount of free processing before charging and costs can rack up quickly
  • Therefore you do not have time to play around or make mistakes- time is money
  • Big Data still requires a degree of competency from the user, it is not something that can just be picked up by anybody
  • The nature of Big Data means you are exporting data externally, potentially bringing in cybersecurity risks
  • It can be challenging to integrate Big Data output into your system
Can apprentices think of any other drawbacks to using Big Data? The big one is the cost- Big Data is not cheap to run, with many solutions offering small free tier limits before ramping up the cost. The potential benefits will often outweigh the costs, but you will need to be confident that the added value is worth it. This will need to be justified to relevant stakeholders before the technology is used. It is paramount then that you are sure the analysis/algorithm will run smoothly, having quality assured your data and code to ensure there are no syntax errors and that the data is clean. Any mistakes could potentially costly to you and your business.

Platforms vs Coding Yourself
Objective: Justify the use of tools by comparing them to coding
Having these tools licensed by your employers is great, but it is not the only way. You have been taught how to use SQL and Python and later on we will be looking at R. When should you code and when should you rely on software? That ultimately is up to you, and whatever you decide it needs to be justified. This section will help you consider the advantages and disadvantages of each.

Advantages

Platforms

  • Easy to use and do not require technical knowledge or skills
  • More agile as 'drag and drop' options allow for quicker building of functions/models/visualisations
  • Greater agility leads to lower costs as less time is spent on development
  • Relatively simple to edit what you have built
  • Often tech support available
  • Accessible to stakeholders who do not have technical coding background

Coding

  • You have complete control over the whole process and can customise colour scheme and functionality in almost any way imaginable
  • Data entry can be faster and more accurate (particularly when automated)
  • Common coding langauges (R, Python, etc) are free to install and use
  • You own the source code
  • Most languages have active communities where you can ask for help
Ask apprentices to suggest some advantages of platforms before revealing them. Draw attention to the ease of use, the tech support and the increased engagement from stakeholders. For coding you can discuss how you have more control over the entire process from data cleaning, analysis to visualisations (and potentially dashboars). Coding also has a supportive community and is also cheap.

Disadvantages

Platforms

  • Can be a steep learning curve and training will be required for each different software you use
  • While platforms often have a wide variety of functions, they are still limited to whatever was built in by the developer
  • Security and reliability issues around you not having complete control over your code/data

Coding

  • Learning to code is a daunting and long process
  • Coding is constantly evolving and it is up to you to keep up with trends- which you may not have time to do
  • Complicated codes take a long time to produce and require constant error checking and validation
Again you can ask apprentices to suggest the disadvantages for both before revealing. Platforms are expensive, limited and can be difficult to use. On top of this you have issues around third parties potentially having access to your data as well as complications around switching platforms (upskilling staff, different formats, etc). Coding has a steep learning curve and is a constantly evolving discipline which requires analysts to keep abreast of. Another issue is around the open source nature of languages where importing libraries can potentilly bring in security risks.

Disadvantages (Cont)

Platforms

  • If you want to change product it can be difficult to migrate your models/visualisations
  • Licenses can be expensive
  • You do not own the source code

Coding

  • Complicated codes take a long time to produce and require constant error checking and validation
  • Easy to make mistakes but can be difficult to find the error
  • Making small changes to large scripts not always simple and can lead to side effects
  • Code is harder to understand for non-technical colleagues
On top of this, platforms limit what you can do and can be expensive. Coding requires constant checks (easy to make mistakes) and is harder to present to stakeholders. Is there anything else we should consider?
